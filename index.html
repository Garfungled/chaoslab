Using the LCE and Dimensionality Reduction Algorithms to
Visually Represent Chaotic Trajectories and Identify Periodic Orbits

An interactive exploration of chaotic dynamical systems using the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent, dimensionality reduction, and GPU-accelerated simulation.

\(\lambda = \lim_{t \to \infty} \dfrac{1}{t} \ln \dfrac{\|\delta(t)\|}{\|\delta(0)\|}\)

Project Overview

This project investigates the structure of chaotic motion in n-pendulum. By computing the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent (LCE) for millions of initial conditions and projecting the results with UMAP, PCA, and t-SNE, we can visually represent the chaotic structure of these systems.

What is the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent?

Two nearby trajectories in a chaotic system diverge exponentially. The LCE \(\lambda\) quantifies that divergence rate. When \(\lambda > 0\), the system is chaotic. When \(\lambda \approx 0\), the orbit is periodic or quasi-periodic. In practice it is estimated by integrating two trajectories simultaneously and periodically rescaling the separation:

\(\lambda \approx \dfrac{1}{T} \sum_{k} \ln \dfrac{\|\delta(t_k)\|}{\|\delta(t_{k-1})\|}\)
\(T\) is the total time of the simulation, \(k\) is the index over each rescaling step, \(\delta(t_k)\) is the seperation between the two trajectories at time \(t_k\), \(\sum_k\) is summing the value \(\dfrac{\|\delta(t_k)\|}{\|\delta(t_{k-1})\|}\) over the index \(k\), \(\text{ln}\) is the natural logarithm function and \(\|\cdot\|\) is the euclidian norm.

Why n-Pendulums?

The n-pendulum is one of the simplest physical systems that exhibits chaos. For \(n = 2\), a two-dimensional heatmap of initial angles already reveals fractal boundaries between chaotic and periodic regions. As \(n\) grows, the parameter space becomes high-dimensional, this is where UMAP becomes essential. Use the navigation above to explore the interactive simulations and heatmaps.
