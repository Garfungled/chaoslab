Chaos, Lyapunov Exponents & Periodic Orbits

An interactive exploration of chaotic dynamical systems using the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent, dimensionality reduction, and GPU-accelerated simulation.

\(\lambda = \lim_{t \to \infty} \dfrac{1}{t} \ln \dfrac{\|\delta(t)\|}{\|\delta(0)\|}\)

Zachary Medjamia — Andover High School Capstone 2026

This project investigates the structure of chaotic motion in n-pendulum and gravitational n-body systems. By computing the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent (LCE) for millions of initial conditions and projecting the results with UMAP, PCA, and t-SNE, the hidden geometry of phase space becomes visible — and rare periodic orbits can be found within it.

What is the Lyapunov Characteristic Exponent?

Two nearby trajectories in a chaotic system diverge exponentially fast. The LCE \(\lambda\) quantifies that divergence rate. When \(\lambda > 0\), the system is chaotic. When \(\lambda \approx 0\), the orbit is periodic or quasi-periodic. In practice it is estimated by integrating two trajectories simultaneously and periodically rescaling the separation:

\(\lambda \approx \dfrac{1}{T} \sum_{k} \ln \dfrac{\|\delta(t_k)\|}{\|\delta(t_{k-1})\|}\)

Why n-Pendulums?

The n-pendulum is one of the simplest physical systems that exhibits genuine chaos. For \(n = 2\), a two-dimensional heatmap of initial angles already reveals fractal boundaries between chaotic and periodic regions. As \(n\) grows, the parameter space becomes high-dimensional — this is where UMAP becomes essential. Use the navigation above to explore the interactive simulations and heatmaps.

All simulations run live in your browser using JavaScript RK4 integration. Click any pixel in the heatmaps to launch that simulation. Scroll to zoom the heatmaps; drag to pan; double-click to reset.
